Las Vegas → Banff

Where you can legally sleep in your vehicle for free (or nearly free) with a lake in front of you and mountains behind you, the whole way north.

Nevada→ Utah→ Idaho→ Montana→ Alberta
The short version

The rule of the road: free south of the border, pay-a-little up north

Almost the entire US leg is genuine free dispersed camping on public land. The catch waits at the finish line: you cannot car camp for free inside Banff itself, or in its gateway town. Here is the whole trip in six lines.

Six things that decide where you sleep

  • US public land is your free bed. BLM land across NV, UT and ID lets you sleep in your vehicle by default. Rule of thumb: 14 days, then move ~25 miles.
  • National forests are the mountain-and-lake sweet spot, free but with per-forest limits (14 days in NV/ID, 16 days around Bozeman, MT).
  • The only vetted named free spots that survived fact-checking cluster in the Bozeman, Montana corridor (Hyalite, Fairy Lake, Copper City).
  • Closest legal free-style camping to Banff = Alberta Crown land, but you need a Public Lands Camping Pass ($20 / 3 days or $30 / year, same price for Americans).
  • Inside Banff & Jasper: car camping is flatly illegal outside paid campgrounds, sleeping-in-vehicle included. Fines and eviction.
  • Canmore (Banff's gateway town) hunts van sleepers: $100 fine 10pm to 8am, no-camping signs at every entrance, active patrols.

Read this before the finish line

The Banff catch: no free car camping at the destination

Everything above gets you free or cheap. The last 100 km is where it flips. Two hard walls stand between you and a free night's sleep at Banff.

⚠️ Wall 1: Inside Banff & Jasper National Parks: illegal, period

Both parks' regulations explicitly prohibit camping including sleeping in a vehicle in roadside pullouts, parking lots, trailheads and day-use areas. Banff's own wording: "Camping is allowed only in designated campgrounds."

There is no free vehicle overnight option inside either park. Frontcountry campgrounds cap at 14 nights and cost money. The only random-camping exception is backcountry, permit-only, and far from any road, so by definition it can never be car camping. Violations draw fines and eviction.

⚠️ Wall 2: Canmore (the gateway town) actively hunts van sleepers

Under Canmore's camping bylaw, sleeping in a vehicle between 10 p.m. and 8 a.m. carries a $100 fine (up to a $10,000 maximum). No-camping signs are posted at every entrance into town, most parking lots are signed no-overnight, and town peace officers actively patrol and contact people sleeping in vehicles.

Canmore runs a paid "Safe Park" program as the sanctioned alternative, but confirm its current price and location directly with the Town, the specific figures floating around online failed fact-checking and are likely stale.

✅ The move that works

Sleep on Alberta Crown land west of Calgary (Eastern Slopes, Public Lands Camping Pass in hand) or in a paid park campground, and day-trip into Banff. Do not plan on a free pullout snooze inside the park or in Canmore, that is the one stretch where "just sleep in the car" gets you ticketed.

The Icefields Parkway

Glacial lakesGlaciers

Often called one of the most scenic drives on Earth: about 230 km (3 hours without stops) linking Banff to Jasper past Peyto Lake, Bow Lake, and the Columbia Icefield. Know these cold

The four rules that govern the whole route

🏕

US BLM, the "14 and move" rule (NV, UT, ID)

Most BLM land allows dispersed camping unless posted "Closed to Camping." Limit is 14 days within any 28-day period at one spot, then relocate, often 25 to 30 miles away. This is the backbone of the free US legs.

🌲

US National Forests, per-forest limits

Dispersed vehicle camping is allowed but each forest sets its own limit. Humboldt-Toiyabe (NV) and Salmon-Challis (ID) use 14 days; Custer Gallatin around Bozeman uses 16 days (32 cumulative per year). Always check the specific forest's order.

🏷️

Alberta Crown land, buy the pass

Random camping in the Eastern Slopes needs a Public Lands Camping Pass for anyone 18+ ($20/3-day or $30/annual, identical rate for non-residents). Max 14 days per spot, then move 1+ km for 72 hours. Kananaskis vehicle access needs the separate Conservation Pass too.

🚫

National parks & Canmore, do not car camp

Banff and Jasper: designated campgrounds only, no vehicle sleeping in pullouts/lots/trailheads. Canmore: $100 fine 10pm to 8am with active patrols. Treat this as a hard no and plan Crown land or a paid campground instead.

Honest gaps

What still needs a boots-on-ground check

The deep-research pass verified every legal rule above against primary government sources, but a few things it could not pin down. Worth a quick call or app-check before you roll:

Open questions the research flagged

  • Specific named lake-view sites for NV, UT and ID. Only the Bozeman, MT cluster produced vetted named spots. For the earlier legs, use apps like iOverlander / The Dyrt / FreeRoam on the ground, anchored to the BLM/NF rules above (Great Basin, Uinta/Wasatch, and the Sawtooth NRA lakes are the scenic targets).
  • Canmore Safe Park current terms. The only sanctioned vehicle-sleeping option near Banff, but its published price/location details failed verification as stale. Confirm with the Town of Canmore directly.
  • Border-crossing practicalities. Firewood, fresh produce, and bear-spray rules crossing into Canada were not verified here, check CBSA before the line. (General tip: don't haul firewood or citrus across; declare bear spray.)
  • Cell coverage & season. Coverage across remote Idaho/Montana and the Eastern Slopes, plus snow closures and fire bans, were not confirmed. Roughly, the free high-country sites are a late-June to mid-September game; download offline maps.
  • Best Alberta PLUZ / BC rec sites within 1 to 2 hrs of Banff for lake-view random camping (Ghost, Waiparous, Spray Lakes vs. pass-gated Kananaskis zones).
