Las Vegas → Crater Lake

Drive out of the desert furnace and up to a 7,000-foot rim where July tops out in the 60s. Free van camping nearly the whole way, straight up the Eastern Sierra and the Cascades.

The short version

Chase the cool air north: free public land the whole way, one paid night at the rim

You asked the right question: Oregon runs a lot cooler. Crater Lake's rim sits above 7,000 feet, so while Vegas bakes, the caldera is jacket weather. Almost the entire drive is free federal land you can sleep on. The one catch waits at the destination, same as always.

Six things that decide where you sleep

  • Almost all of it is free. BLM desert, then a long chain of national forests up US-395 and the Cascades. Rule of thumb on both: 14 days, then move on.
  • Alabama Hills changed the rules. The famous Lone Pine boulder field now allows camping only in ~50 marked sites, and you must carry a free permit (7 days per 28, all first-come first-served).
  • The Eastern Sierra is your cool, scenic middle. Inyo National Forest allows dispersed camping (14 days), but not in the developed zones: Mammoth Lakes Basin, June Lake Loop, and the Mono Lake shore are campground-only.
  • Closest free bed to Crater Lake = Umpqua / Rogue-Siskiyou forest roads off Highway 62 and around Diamond Lake. Standard 14-day dispersed, and it is genuinely a few minutes from the park's west entrance.
  • Inside Crater Lake: no vehicle camping. Sleeping in pullouts, lots, or trailheads is prohibited. Overnight means Mazama Campground (~$35+ a night, reservations Jul–Oct) or a backcountry permit.
  • Two hazards bracket the trip: Death Valley in July is genuinely deadly (120°+, stay on pavement), and the Crater Lake Rim Drive doesn't fully open until mid-July most years.

⚠️ Before you roll: the first 200 miles can kill you in July

The direct line out of Vegas skirts Death Valley, and summer there is not a photo op you improvise. 2024 averaged a 117°F high with 37 days over 120°F. The Park Service rules are blunt: drink a gallon of water a day, stay on paved roads so rangers can find you, and never leave a broken-down vehicle.

Practical move: treat Death Valley as a dawn drive-through or skip it entirely by running US-95 to Beatty and over to US-395. Save the sightseeing for the cool high country ahead. Camping in the valley is only sane at the high-elevation campgrounds, and roadside sleeping on paved roads and pullouts is banned there anyway.

Read this before the finish line

The Crater Lake catch: no free van camping at the destination

Everything above gets you free or nearly free. Inside the park, that flips. Two things to plan around: where you're allowed to sleep, and whether the rim road is even open yet.

⚠️ Wall 1: Inside the park, sleeping in your vehicle is prohibited

Crater Lake's rules are the standard national-park line: overnight only in a designated campground or the backcountry with a permit. Sleeping in pullouts, parking lots, and trailheads is not allowed. There is no free vehicle option inside the boundary.

Your two legal beds: Mazama Campground (214 sites, tent/no-hookup roughly $35 and up, hookups more) open about May 22 to Sept 30, first-come late May through June then reservation-required July 1 to Oct 1 via recreation.gov; or the smaller Lost Creek Campground. Entry is $30 per vehicle in summer, good for 7 days. Easiest free play: sleep on the Umpqua/Diamond Lake forest roads above and day-trip in.

⚠️ Wall 2: The Rim Drive opens late, so time it

This is a high, snowy caldera. Plowing starts in April, but the full 33-mile Rim Drive typically isn't open until mid-July, and it closes with the first big storm (often around Nov 1). The West Rim usually opens first (mid-May to late June); the East Rim can hold snow into late July.

If you want the whole loop and both entrances, aim for mid-July through mid-October. Earlier than that and you may reach the rim but be turned around halfway around the lake.

✅ The move that works

Sleep free on the Umpqua / Rogue-Siskiyou forest roads near the west entrance (Union Creek, Diamond Lake), or book Mazama if you want to be inside. Roll up to the rim for sunrise, drive the loop, and do not plan on a free pullout snooze inside the park. Go mid-July or later so the whole Rim Drive is open.

Know these cold

The four rules that govern the whole route

🏕

US BLM, the "14 and move" rule (NV desert)

Most BLM land allows dispersed camping unless posted "Closed to Camping." Limit is 14 days within any 28-day period at one spot, then relocate, often 25 to 30 miles. This is the backbone of the free Nevada leg out of Vegas.

🌲

US National Forests, per-forest limits

Dispersed vehicle camping is allowed but each forest sets its own order. Inyo, Fremont-Winema, Umpqua, and Rogue-Siskiyou use 14 days; Plumas is stricter (14 per district, 30/year). Developed zones (Mammoth Basin, Mono shore, Cascade Lakes corridor) are carved out, so read the signs.

🔥

California, carry a free campfire permit

On federal land in California, a free California Campfire Permit is required for any open flame outside a developed campground, including camp stoves and lanterns, not just wood fires. Get it in two minutes online (preventwildfiresca.org). It doesn't override seasonal fire bans, which can outlaw wood fires entirely.

🚫

National parks & monuments, no vehicle sleeping

Crater Lake and Lassen Volcanic: designated campgrounds only, no sleeping in pullouts, lots, or trailheads. Lava Beds: Indian Well Campground only. Alabama Hills: marked sites plus a permit. Treat all four as a hard no on free roadside sleeping and use the forest land nearby.

Honest gaps

What still needs a boots-on-ground check

Every legal rule above was verified against primary government sources. A few things move with the season or the field office, so confirm these before you commit:

Open questions the research flagged

  • Rim Drive and Mazama opening dates for your week. Snow decides both. Check the Crater Lake NPS "hours & roads" page and current reservations before you count on the full loop or a campsite.
  • Lava Beds campground fee. The Park Service doesn't publish the nightly number; recent visitors report about $20 via a pay machine. Confirm on arrival and bring a card.
  • Named dispersed pullouts near Highway 62. The Union Creek / River Bridge / Diamond Lake spots come from crowd sources (iOverlander, The Dyrt, Campendium). Great starting points, but read the signs on the ground; some corridors get seasonal closures.
  • Fire restrictions. By mid-summer, Stage 1 or 2 bans are common across these forests and can outlaw all open flame. Check each forest's alerts page the week you travel.
  • Death Valley routing. If you insist on driving through in July, do it at dawn with a full tank, extra water, and a working A/C. Otherwise take the US-95 bypass through Beatty.
